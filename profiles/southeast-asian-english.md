# Southeast Asian English — Working Profile

> **This is a starting point, not a definitive description.** Southeast Asia contains many
> distinct English-using communities — Singaporean, Malaysian, Filipino, Burmese, Indonesian,
> Thai, Vietnamese — each with its own history, contact languages, and norms. This profile
> sketches *shared written tendencies* documented in the linguistics literature on the region's
> Englishes, with particular attention to Myanmar English and Colloquial Singapore English
> ("Singlish"). It is meant to be forked. If you write in any of these registers, edit it
> to fit your own voice; it is not meant to fit you on first contact.

---

## 1. Identity

**Profile name:** Southeast Asian English — Working Profile
**Intended scope:** Written English influenced by SE Asian first languages, used in
semi-formal to professional digital contexts (blog posts, long-form messages, drafts that
will be edited downward into formal documents). Draws most heavily on documented features
of Myanmar English and Colloquial Singapore English; readers and writers from Malaysia, the
Philippines, Indonesia, Thailand, and Vietnam will find overlap and divergence.
**Out of scope:** Strictly formal academic prose, edited journalism, spoken/oral varieties,
fiction, the deeply colloquial registers of any one local variety (e.g. full basilectal
Singlish — see Leimgruber 2011), and any one writer's personal idiolect.

## 2. Disclaimer

This profile is shaped by published linguistics — Kachru's three-circles framework, Schneider's
postcolonial Englishes model, and the descriptive literature on regional varieties — plus the
specific work cited in the bibliography. It does not claim to describe how *anyone* writes; it
describes patterns that *recur* in the literature, which a humanising model can be told to
preserve rather than smooth away. Communities whose voices this profile attempts to describe
are invited to edit it.

## 3. Voice signatures — PRESERVE

- **Topic-prominent sentence openings** — fronting the topic before the predicate, sometimes
  with no explicit subject in the comment clause.
  - Example: *That meeting yesterday, very hard to follow.*
- **Copula deletion / variable copula** — the verb "to be" omitted where the topic and
  predicate are juxtaposed. Especially common in CSE; documented in Leimgruber (2011) and
  Lim (2007).
  - Example: *The deadline very tight.*
- **Aspect-over-tense marking** — preference for marking completion or ongoing-ness rather
  than strict time reference. Influenced by aspectual systems of Burmese, Sinitic, Malay.
  - Example: *I already send the report.* / *We still discussing.*
- **Reduplication for emphasis or softening** — repeating a word or short phrase to
  intensify, soften, or pluralise (documented in Burmese; see Watkins / Bradley).
  - Example: *Small small things keep coming up.*
- **Sentence-final discourse particles in semi-formal writing** — *lah, lor, leh, meh, hor,
  sia* and equivalents, when register permits. These are not noise; they carry mood,
  obviousness, contrast, resignation (Wee 2018; Lim 2007). Whether to *preserve* them in any
  given document depends on the target register — this profile flags them as signal, not error.
- **Honorific naming and kinship terms used in professional address** — *U, Daw, Auntie,
  Uncle, Sir/Madam, Sayama, Teacher, Ate, Kuya, Po, Bro* — used as politeness anchors with
  social-hierarchy meaning, not as familiarities. (Discussed in Bautista & Bolton 2008;
  Burmese honorifics in Watkins 2010.)
  - Example: *Daw Khin will join us at 3.* / *Po, I just wanted to confirm.*
- **Article variability** — definite and indefinite articles used where a Standard
  British/American writer might omit them, or omitted where one might include them. This is
  systematic, not error (Platt, Weber & Ho 1984; Schneider 2007 on Phase 3/4 varieties).
- **Light directness, heavy politeness** — short, plain sentences carrying the request,
  wrapped in extensive politeness framing. Don't smooth the politeness away; don't pad the
  request.
- **Code-mixed lexical items where the local word carries meaning the English equivalent
  lacks** — e.g. *kiasu, paiseh, jialat, sayang* in CSE writing; *nway, ma shi bu, ah-mei*
  in Myanmar English; *naka, opo* in Filipino English. Treat as content words.
- **Zero anaphora across short clauses** — the subject understood from prior clause without
  pronoun. Documented for Burmese discourse (see PMC 2021 review of Asian pragmatic particles).
- **"Already" as completion marker** — not redundant; functioning as an aspectual particle.
  - Example: *I send to you already.*

## 4. AI-smoothing patterns — REVERSE

- **Forced Standard-English copula insertion** — automated editors insert *is/are/was/were*
  to "complete" topic-comment structures.
  - Smoothed: *The deadline is very tight, and we need to coordinate.*
  - Restored: *The deadline very tight. We need to coordinate.*
- **Tense-correction over aspect** — past-tense forms substituted for completive *already*.
  - Smoothed: *I sent the report yesterday.*
  - Restored: *I already send the report.*
- **Deletion of sentence-final particles** — *lah, leh, lor, sia* and similar stripped as
  "informal" even where the writer chose them deliberately.
  - Smoothed: *That's fine.*
  - Restored: *That's fine lah.*
- **Honorifics flattened to first names** — *U Aung* becomes *Aung*, *Ate Lily* becomes *Lily*.
  Restore the honorific.
- **Politeness framing trimmed** — "Just wanted to confirm if it's okay…" compressed to
  "Confirming…". This loses face-work that the writer encoded on purpose.
- **Article normalisation** — articles added or deleted to match British/American norms,
  erasing systematic regional patterns.
- **Reduplication unrolled** — *small small* → *small* or *minor*. Lossy.
- **Code-mixed words translated** — *kiasu* → *competitive*; *paiseh* → *embarrassed*.
  Often loses precision.

## 5. Sentence rhythm

- Median sentence length: 10–16 words.
- Range: 3–30 words, with more short sentences than typical AI-smoothed prose.
- Rhythm note: short topic-comment openings tend to be followed by one or two longer
  explanatory sentences. Listy paragraphs of similar-length sentences are uncommon in
  authentic SE Asian English writing; AI smoothing tends to introduce that uniformity.

## 6. Lexical preferences

- **Prefer:** *already* (completive), *lah/leh/lor* (where register allows), kinship/honorific
  address, region-specific content words, *the same / one / that one* as anaphora, *only*
  (focus particle, often clause-final).
- **Avoid (when smoothing):** *moreover, furthermore, additionally, in conclusion*,
  over-nominalised abstractions (*the implementation of the consideration*), British/American
  idiom drop-ins (*at the end of the day, going forward*) unless the writer already uses them.

## 7. Discourse markers

- *Actually* — used to introduce real / contrastive information, often clause-initial.
- *Like that* — anaphoric "in that way".
- *Or what* — alternative-question marker at clause end.
- *Got* — existential ("There is/are…"). *Got problem ah?*
- *Can / Cannot* — bare modal as full response. *Can.* *Cannot, sorry.*
- Reduplicated *very very, small small, slowly slowly* — intensification or softening.

## 8. Punctuation and orthography

Capitalisation tends toward Standard rules in professional writing across the region. Em-dashes
are uncommon; commas and short sentences carry the same load. Parentheses are common for
clarifying acronyms and Romanised names. Do not impose Oxford commas or em-dashes where the
writer used a period.

## 9. What this profile is NOT trying to do

This is not a profile for full basilectal Singlish (use Leimgruber 2011 and Wee 2018 as primary
sources if that is the target). It is not a profile for spoken English from any SE Asian
country. It is not a profile for academic writing — academic registers in Singapore, the
Philippines, and Myanmar tend toward more standardised forms (see Bolton 2019). It is not a
description of any single national variety; treat it as a shared scaffold to fork from.

## 10. Sample restoration

**Before (smoothed):**

> Thank you for your email. I have reviewed the proposal carefully, and I think there are
> several issues that need to be addressed before we can move forward. First, the deadline
> is very tight. Second, the budget allocation is unclear. I will send a detailed response
> tomorrow.

**After (restored):**

> Thank you for your email. I already go through the proposal — got a few things to discuss
> before we can move forward. The deadline very tight. Budget allocation also not so clear.
> Tomorrow I send detailed response.

(Note: the "after" leans slightly Singlish-coloured. A Myanmar English version would keep
*already* as completion marker but drop the sentence-final particles, and might use *U/Daw*
honorifics in the address. This is the point of forking.)

## 11. Annotated bibliography

- **Kachru, B. B. (1985, 1992).** *The Other Tongue: English Across Cultures.* — Provides
  the three-circles framework (Inner / Outer / Expanding) the profile uses to place SE Asian
  Englishes. Myanmar is typically positioned in the Expanding Circle; Singapore, Malaysia,
  the Philippines in the Outer Circle.
- **Schneider, E. W. (2007).** *Postcolonial English: Varieties Around the World.* Cambridge
  University Press. — The dynamic model (Phases 1–5) explains why article systems, copula
  use, and tense/aspect marking pattern differently in different SE Asian Englishes.
- **Platt, J., Weber, H., & Ho, M. L. (1984).** *The New Englishes.* Routledge. — Foundational
  description of grammatical features (copula variability, article use, tense/aspect) shared
  across SE Asian Englishes. Cited throughout the profile's signatures.
- **Bolton, K. (Ed.) (2019).** *Asian Englishes* (journal). Routledge. — Ongoing venue for
  descriptive work on Asian varieties. Useful for current-decade citations.
- **Leimgruber, J. R. E. (2011).** "Singapore English." *Language and Linguistics Compass* 5(1).
  — Concise survey of CSE phonology, lexis, and syntax, including topic prominence, topic-comment.
- **Lim, L. (2007).** "Mergers and acquisitions: On the ages and origins of Singapore English
  particles." *World Englishes* 26(4). — Catalogues particles.
- **Wee, L. (2018).** *The Singlish Controversy.* Cambridge University Press. — Treats particles.
- **Deterding, D. (2007).** *Singapore English.* Edinburgh University Press. — Rhythm.
- **Bautista, M. L. S., & Bolton, K. (2008).** *Philippine English.*  HKUP. — Philippine.
- **Watkins, J. (2010, 2013).** Burmese phonology/grammar. — Burmese honorifics.
- **Tan, B. T. (2014).** Myanmar English education policy.
- **Bolton, K. (2019).** "Kachru and Asian Englishes." — Myanmar place.
- **Hashim, A., & Leitner, G. (2016).** *Communicating with Asia.* — Code-mixing.
- **Ling & Deterding (2007).** Singlish particle function.
- **PMC review (2021).** Particles: Findings From Asian Languages.
