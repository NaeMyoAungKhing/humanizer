# American Business English — Plain-Language Tradition

> **This is a starting point, not a definitive description of how Americans write at work.**
> The American business-writing landscape contains many traditions: the dense, nominalised
> corporate register described by Biber's corpus studies; the plain-language tradition
> codified by Williams, Garner, and Kimble; the Hemingway/Kansas-City-Star inheritance.
> This profile leans toward the plain-language end. Fork it freely.

---

## 1. Identity

**Profile name:** American Business English — Plain-Language Tradition
**Intended scope:** Professional written English in US corporate, non-profit, and government
contexts. Emails, memos, briefs, proposals, internal docs. Mid-formal register.
**Out of scope:** Legal contracts, regulatory filings, marketing copy, academic prose, fiction,
regional dialects, and writing in languages other than English.

## 2. Disclaimer

This profile is shaped by the plain-language literature (Williams, Garner, Kimble),
Biber's register-corpus work, and the broader Hemingway/Strunk lineage in American writing
instruction. It is not a description of how all Americans write at work — much American
corporate prose is, in fact, the opposite of this profile. It describes the *intended* end
state of the plain-language tradition, which is the register most worth restoring when AI
smoothing pulls a draft toward boilerplate.

## 3. Voice signatures — PRESERVE

- **Subject-verb-object early in the sentence.** The reader should know who is doing what
  within the first six or seven words. (Williams, *Style*, on "characters and actions".)
- **Active voice as the default, passive when the agent is unknown, irrelevant, or
  deliberately suppressed.** Active is not always right, but it is the default.
- **Short sentences interleaved with longer ones.** The Kansas City Star style book that
  shaped Hemingway: "Use short sentences. Use short first paragraphs. Use vigorous English."
  Variation.
- **Concrete nouns and action verbs over abstractions.** *We shipped the rollout* over
  *The rollout was executed*.
- **Direct requests with one verb of asking.** *Can you send the deck by Thursday?*
- **Numbers where numbers belong.** Dates, deadlines, dollar figures, percentages — stated
  plainly, not couched.
- **BLUF — bottom line up front.** One idea per paragraph; first sentence carries the point.
- **Plain transitions: *but, so, also, then.*** Not *moreover, furthermore, additionally*.
- **First person where the writer is the agent.** *I recommend* rather than *It is recommended*.
- **Specific names, dates, dollar amounts as anchors.** Restore them when smoothed away.

## 4. AI-smoothing patterns — REVERSE

- **Nominalisation creep.** (See Kies 1985; Williams.)
  - Smoothed: *The implementation of the new policy resulted in a reduction of complaints.*
  - Restored: *We rolled out the new policy. Complaints dropped.*
- **Passive-voice insertion where an agent exists.** *A decision was made* → *We decided*.
- **Hedge stacking.** *It seems that perhaps we may want to consider possibly delaying* →
  *We should probably delay.*
- **Latinate replacement of plain verbs.** *Utilize, leverage, facilitate, optimize,
  ascertain, endeavor, commence* → *use, use, help, improve, find out, try, start*.
- **Transitional padding.** *In conclusion, going forward, it is important to note that*.
- **Bullet inflation. Equal-length sentences. First-person erasure.**

## 5. Sentence rhythm

- Median sentence length: 12–18 words.
- Range: 3–35 words. Wide range is signal; narrow range is smoothing.
- Rhythm note: a short declarative sentence followed by a longer supporting one is the
  characteristic two-beat of plain-language business prose.

## 6. Lexical preferences

- **Prefer:** *use, help, start, end, decide, ship, send, ask, plan, fix, build, try, want,
  need, this, that, we, I, you.*
- **Avoid (when smoothing imposed them):** *utilize, leverage, facilitate, robust, holistic,
  synergy, paradigm, ecosystem, journey, unlock, empower, seamless, scalable, best-in-class,
  thought leadership, value-add, granular, deep dive, actionable, going forward, at the end
  of the day, in conclusion, it is important to note that.*

## 7. Discourse markers

- *But, so, and, also, then, because.* Sentence-initial use allowed (Garner).
- *Here's the thing.* Set up a clarification.

## 8. Punctuation and orthography

- Em-dashes common; semicolons uncommon.
- Oxford comma conventional in formal documents; optional in email.
- Currency/dates/percentages: *$1.2M, Q3 2026, 14%*.

## 9. What this profile is NOT trying to do

This is not "how Americans talk". Not for legal contracts. Not for marketing copy. Does not
capture regional variation within US English. It is the plain-language tradition — the
register AI smoothing drifts *away* from toward generic corporate prose.

## 10. Sample restoration

**Before (smoothed):**

> In the rapidly evolving landscape of customer success, it has become increasingly important
> for organizations to leverage data-driven insights in order to facilitate meaningful
> improvements to the customer journey.

**After (restored):**

> Customers churn faster than we can onboard them. The team is going to fix that. We're
> shipping three things next quarter: a clearer onboarding email, a help-doc rewrite, and
> a thirty-day check-in call. I'll send the plan Friday.

## 11. Annotated bibliography

- **Williams, J. M., & Bizup, J. (2017).** *Style: Lessons in Clarity and Grace.* Pearson.
  — The "characters and actions" framework. Foundational for §3 and §4.
- **Garner, B. A. (2013).** *Legal Writing in Plain English.* Univ. of Chicago Press. —
  Defines plain language. Source for verb-list and sentence-initial *but/so/and*.
- **Garner, B. A. (2016).** *Garner's Modern English Usage.* Oxford UP. — Latinate/plain
  word pairs.
- **Strunk, W., & White, E. B. (1999).** *The Elements of Style.* — Cited as lineage.
- **Hemingway / Kansas City Star Style Book (1915, ongoing).** Short-sentence default.
- **Kimble, J. (2006).** *Lifting the Fog of Legalese.* Carolina Academic Press.
- **Biber, D. (1988).** *Variation Across Speech and Writing.* Cambridge UP. — MD-A.
- **Biber, D., & Conrad, S. (2009).** *Register, Genre, and Style.* Cambridge UP.
- **Friginal, E. (2018).** "Corpus Analysis of Business English." Wiley.

- **plainlanguage.gov.** US federal Plain Writing Act.
- **Pullum, G. K. (2009).** "50 Years of Stupid Grammar Advice." Chronicle.
- **Schiess, W. (2003).** "What Plain English Really Is." *Scribes Journal of Legal Writing.*
